No contact rule after breakup

No contact rule after breakup: what counts, how long, and what to do first.

Use this guide to define the no contact rule clearly, choose a first no-contact window, and know what to do when the urge to text your ex gets loud.

What the no contact rule is for

The no contact rule is not a trick to make someone miss you. It is a clear boundary that gives your mind and body time to come down from the breakup. When every message restarts the emotional loop, no contact creates enough quiet to notice what you actually need.

Quick answer: the no contact rule means no direct texting, calling, replying, reacting, checking for reassurance, or sending messages through other people unless there is a real logistics or safety reason.

What counts as contact?

Direct communication usually counts: texting, calling, replying, reacting to stories, sending voice notes, meeting up to process the breakup, or asking friends to pass messages. If the action reopens the connection or invites a response, treat it as contact.

Checking their profile may not be direct contact, but it can still keep you emotionally attached. If social media keeps resetting your mood, use the social media boundary guide too.

Usually counts as contact

Texts, calls, voice notes, replies, story reactions, emotional check-ins, meetups to process the breakup, or messages through friends.

Usually allowed exceptions

Brief logistics about housing, bills, shared items, parenting, safety, legal matters, or unavoidable work responsibilities.

How long is the no contact rule?

Most people need a first window, not a forever promise. Start with 14 days if the breakup is fresh and daily life still overlaps. Start with 30 days if every contact restarts hope, anxiety, or checking. If you are unsure, read how long should I do no contact and use the no contact calculator to make the count visible.

A simple first plan

  • Choose a start date: use your last direct contact date.
  • Pick a first window: start with 14 or 30 days instead of promising forever.
  • Write one exception list: shared bills, housing, children, safety, or logistics only.
  • Track the streak: make progress visible so one hard night does not erase the whole week.

What to do when you want to text

Do not negotiate with the urge at its peak. Put the exact message in an unsent note, wait 60 seconds, then check whether the message solves a real problem or just asks for relief. If you need help right now, use want to text your ex or why do I want to text my ex.

When no contact is not total silence

No contact is a recovery boundary, not a reason to ignore real responsibilities. If you share children, housing, bills, legal matters, pets, work, or safety concerns, keep the message short and practical. Do not use logistics as a doorway into the breakup conversation.

If you break no contact

One message is a restart point, not proof that you failed. Stop the follow-up spiral, write down what triggered you, and restart the count from the next clean choice. Read broke no contact if you need a reset plan.

Frequently asked questions

Should I block my ex during no contact?

Block or mute if seeing their name makes you spiral, if they keep reaching out, or if checking becomes compulsive. Blocking can be a boundary, not a punishment.

How long is the no contact rule?

A common starting point is 14 to 30 days. Choose the length based on how much contact reopens the emotional loop, then review from a calmer place.

Does watching stories break no contact?

Watching is not always direct contact, but reacting, replying, or checking repeatedly can keep you attached. If it makes you spiral, treat it as a boundary problem.

Can I respond if my ex texts me?

You can respond to necessary logistics. Keep it short, neutral, and specific. If the message is emotional, wait before replying and read what to do if your ex texts you.

Can I use no contact if we share responsibilities?

Yes, but keep contact limited to logistics. Use short, neutral messages and avoid breakup processing inside practical conversations.

Is no contact always the right choice?

No. Safety, legal issues, parenting, housing, and health needs can require contact. This site is a self-help tool, not legal, medical, or crisis advice.
